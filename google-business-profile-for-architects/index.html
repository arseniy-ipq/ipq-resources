Google Business Profile for architects

About 18,100 times a month, someone types "architect near me" and Google answers with a map and three names. That shortlist is assembled by an algorithm, costs nothing to enter, and most firms have never once opened the profile that decides whether they are on it.

Arseniy VolosevichFounder, IPQmedia·Updated Jul 14, 2026·7 min read

We build paid pipelines for architecture firms, so a free channel is an odd thing for us to open with. This one earns it. The map sits above the rankings your SEO for architects work is grinding toward, entry is a profile that takes an afternoon, and most of your competitors have abandoned theirs.

The short answer

To get an architecture firm into Google's map pack: claim the profile, pick the most specific category, fill every field, load real project photos, and collect reviews on a schedule, asked for at each project's happiest week. Profile signals and reviews together drive roughly half the local ranking. Residential firms get leads straight off the map. Commercial firms get their due-diligence check passed, which is quieter and worth just as much.

The shortlist Google runs without you

Search anything with local intent, "architect near me" or "residential architect" plus your city, and Google plants a map near the top of the page with three businesses under it. The local pack. Those three get the profile visits, the calls, and the quiet authority of looking like the answer. Position four might as well be page nine.

Now the uncomfortable part. Google builds a profile for your firm whether you participate or not, assembled from whatever the internet happens to say about you. Unclaimed, that can mean a map pin pointing at the dry cleaner next door, hours you have never kept, and one photo of your office door from 2019. Buyers assume you chose all of it.

"But Maps is for plumbers"

That is the standard objection, and the numbers disagree. "Architect near me" pulls about 18,100 US searches a month, with "architects near me" and "architecture firms near me" adding roughly 12,100 each. The same click costs around nine dollars when you buy it as an ad. The map hands it to you free.

If you do residential work, the map is a direct lead source. A homeowner types "architect near me" late in the journey, usually with land bought and a builder already in their ear, which makes them one of the most qualified strangers who will ever go looking for you. Three firms get shown. Everyone else is, functionally, closed.

If you do commercial work, developers do not pick architects off a map, and it would worry us if they did. Your profile works a different shift: due diligence. Search your firm's name and the profile takes over the right side of the screen. BrightLocal's 2025 survey found 88 percent of consumers use Google to find reviews of local businesses, and a developer vetting a shortlist behaves the same way, just with a bigger spreadsheet.

It is the same Google check that quietly filters your referrals, the one we covered in referral systems for architects: your name comes up at dinner, and before dessert someone has searched it. The profile is what they find. Empty is an answer too. And whatever the profile promises, the click lands on your website next, where website design for architects decides whether the visit survives.

What decides who makes the pack

Google names three factors: relevance, distance, and prominence. In Whitespark's 2026 survey of local search experts, profile signals carry 32 percent of the local pack decision and reviews another 20 percent. Half the game lives inside a profile most firms opened once, at gunpoint, in 2021. And the parts you control are not mysterious.

1. Pick the category like money depends on it

Your primary category is one of the heaviest signals you get to choose. Pick the most specific one Google offers for what you actually sell, not the vaguest one that felt safe. Then make the rest of the profile agree with it: the services list, the description, the service area. Write them for a buyer, not a search engine. Keyword stuffing reads as desperate to both.

2. Fill in every field Google gives you

Google's own guidance says complete profiles are more likely to show up in local results, which is about as close as Google gets to handing out an answer key. Address or service area, hours, description, services, real contact details. It is unglamorous data entry, it takes one afternoon, and it is the cheapest ranking work you will ever do.

3. Run reviews on a schedule, not on luck

Reviews are a fifth of the ranking and most of the persuasion, and they respond to systems. Ask at the peak: move-in week for residential clients, opening day or the first occupancy numbers for commercial ones. Then answer every review you get. Only 42 percent of people now trust reviews as much as a personal recommendation, down from 79 in 2020, so buyers read them skeptically, and a considered owner reply is the part that reads as real.

4. Post photos of finished work

You sell the way buildings look, and the default photos on an unclaimed profile are whatever Street View saw that day. Load finished projects and keep loading them as work completes, because a profile that visibly moves reads as a firm that is busy. No stock, no renders passed off as builds. Buyers can tell.

5. Do not get clever with addresses

A fake office to enter a second city's pack is the local version of buying backlinks: it works until Google notices, then the whole profile gets suspended while your real leads bounce. If you work from home, set the profile up as a service-area business and hide the address. Eligible, honest, and it keeps your living room off the internet.

Where the map stops working

The pack rewards proximity, not portfolio. Distance is one of Google's three factors, and no amount of optimizing moves your office. You compete inside a radius Google decides, for whoever happens to search this week, ranked by an algorithm that offers you no dials. You cannot show up to more buyers than exist, and you cannot choose which ones.

That is the gap between capturing demand and creating it. The profile catches people already looking for an architect nearby. It does nothing about the buyers who have not started searching, homeowners quietly planning a build if you do residential, developers and business owners with a project taking shape if you do commercial. Reaching them first, at a volume you control, is the job of paid ads, and the full case is in Meta ads for architects.

So run both, with expectations in the right order: the profile is free capture, set up once and maintained lightly, and the paid engine is where growth actually gets decided. How the two slot into one system is mapped in architecture marketing and built out in lead generation for architects.

A few honest answers

Can you pay Google for a better local ranking?

Not the organic pack. Google's words: "There's no way to request or pay for a better local ranking on Google." What money can buy is the sponsored slot that sometimes sits above the map, which is a different game with different math, covered in Google Ads for architects.

How many reviews do I need?

More than the three firms Google currently shows instead of you, and fresher. There is no magic number. A steady trickle beats a pile from 2022, because recency is part of what Google and buyers both read. Two reviews a quarter, asked for at the happiest moment of each project, can quietly pass firms twice your age.

I do commercial work across three metros. Does any of this matter?

The pack matters least for you. The profile still matters. You will not map-pack in a city where you have no real address, and faking one gets the profile suspended. But every developer who hears your name runs the brand search, and a profile with reviews from past clients is what answers it. Treat it as your due diligence page, not your lead source, and let the outbound engine do the traveling. Press coverage works the same shift, which is PR and press for architects.

An afternoon of setup, a photo habit, a review ask built into project closeout, and you are ahead of most firms in your metro on a channel that costs nothing but attention.
