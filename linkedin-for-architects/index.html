LinkedIn for architects

LinkedIn has more than a billion members, and roughly 310 million of them show up in any given month. Every one is there in work mode. That single fact decides whether the platform is worth your Tuesday, and for exactly half of firms the answer is no.

Arseniy VolosevichFounder, IPQmedia·Updated Jul 14, 2026·8 min read

Nobody opens LinkedIn to plan the biggest personal purchase of their life. They open it to hire, get hired, sell, or look busy between the two. So before you spend a single evening writing posts, the only question that matters is whether the people who commission your work are ever in that headspace. For some firms they live there. For others they are a thousand miles away, on a completely different platform, thinking about their house.

The short answer

LinkedIn works for architects only when the client is a business. Commercial firms can stay visible to the developers and owners who build shortlists, and the platform drives most of the B2B leads that come from social. For residential firms it is a staff room and a rolodex, not a client source, because nobody plans their house in work mode. Either way it is a supporting layer: a few posts a week from the principal, never the budget or hours that belong to the engine.

Get that one distinction right and LinkedIn becomes a genuinely useful layer in the system mapped in architecture marketing. Get it wrong and you will spend a year performing thought leadership for an audience of other architects and recruiters, then quietly conclude that marketing does not work. It worked. You were just in the wrong room.

Which half of the market are you in

This is the whole page, so we will not bury it. LinkedIn is a professional network, which makes it a business-to-business tool. Your firm may sell to businesses or it may sell to people spending their own money on their own home. Those are two different worlds, and LinkedIn only opens the door to one of them.

If you do residential work

Your client is a homeowner, and a homeowner does not go to LinkedIn to find someone to design their house. They go to Instagram, to Houzz, to Google, and to whoever a friend mentioned at dinner. Posting your latest project to a professional network in the hope a future client scrolls past is fishing in an empty pond. If reaching those buyers early is the job, the actual playbook is in marketing for residential architects, and none of it happens here.

That does not mean delete your account. LinkedIn still earns a small slice of a residential firm's attention for two jobs that have nothing to do with winning clients: staying visible to the builders, interior designers, and realtors who send you work, and hiring the next architect you need. Treat it as a staff room and a rolodex, not a shopfront, and it pays for the hour a week it costs.

If you do commercial work

Now you are in the right room. Your client is a developer, a business owner, a facilities director, or the person quietly assembling a shortlist, and a lot of them genuinely are on LinkedIn, in work mode, when your name could land in front of them. This is the one place the platform's B2B reputation is earned.

LinkedIn's own often-cited figure is that it drives about 80 percent of the business-to-business leads that come from social media, and for commercial firms that statistic finally points at your buyer.

Read the small print, though. That is 80 percent of the leads that come from social, and social is a thin slice of how commercial work actually gets awarded. LinkedIn keeps you known and credible with the people who make shortlists. It is slow, relationship-paced, and it will not hand you a signed project on a schedule you choose. The full commercial approach, including where the reliable volume comes from, is in marketing for commercial architects.

What LinkedIn is actually good at

For the firms it fits, LinkedIn does a few things well, and none of them is generating demand on command. It keeps you top of mind with the person who will build a shortlist eight months from now, so when the project appears you are already a name they trust rather than a cold search result.

It feeds the people who feed you. The developers, contractors, brokers, and past clients who refer work are the same professionals scrolling the feed, and staying visible to them is how a referral network stays warm instead of going cold between jobs.

Running that network on purpose is its own discipline, covered in referral systems for architects, and LinkedIn is one of the quieter tools inside it. When those relationships harden into standing arrangements with a defined handoff, that's partnerships for architects.

It also builds the boring but real signal that you are a legitimate, active firm. A principal who writes clearly about their own projects looks like exactly what a nervous commercial client wants: someone who has done this before and can explain it. That is reputation, and reputation shortens the sales cycle even when it never generates a lead you can trace.

What to post, and what to stop posting

Almost every firm on LinkedIn posts the same thing, and it is the wrong thing. "Thrilled to announce" the award, the new hire, the ribbon cutting. It is a press release written for peers, and peers do not commission buildings. Nobody outside the profession has ever changed their mind about a firm because it won a plaque.

Post what a client would actually find useful. The decision behind a design, the part of a project that nearly went sideways and how you handled it, what a type of building really costs to run, why one site constraint changed everything.

The knowledge you consider obvious is what a developer vetting firms cannot get anywhere else, and it does the persuading that adjectives cannot. The long-form version of the same knowledge, parked on your own site where it also ranks, is content marketing for architects.

Two mechanics worth knowing. First, people follow people, not logos: a company page reaches only about 5 to 10 percent of its followers per post and averages around a 2.5 percent engagement rate, while a principal posting from their own face does far better. Put the firm's voice in a human.

Second, consistency beats volume. Three real posts a week outperform seven filler ones, and the firms that post a few times weekly grow their audience meaningfully faster than the ones who post daily noise or vanish for a month.

Where LinkedIn stops

Here is the ceiling, and it is a hard one. LinkedIn is an awareness and relationship channel. It makes you known, trusted, and remembered. It does not let you decide on a slow Tuesday that you want six more qualified inquiries and then produce them. Awareness is not acquisition, and confusing the two is how firms end up busy on social and quiet in the pipeline.

Creating demand at a volume you choose, for both residential and commercial firms, is the job of paid advertising, and the case for the platform that does it best is in Meta ads for architects. That is the engine.

LinkedIn, at its best, is one of the things that makes the engine convert better: the developer who saw your posts for months is warmer when your ad finally reaches them, and the credibility you built is what closes the call. It compounds slowly, like the trust layer it belongs to, and it works alongside the paid engine, never instead of it.

So the order of operations is simple. Run the engine that brings buyers on purpose, then use LinkedIn to stay known to the ones who take months to decide. How the whole pipeline snaps together is in lead generation for architects.

A few honest answers

Are LinkedIn ads worth it for architects?

They can work, and the targeting is genuinely precise: you can aim at a job title and a company size in a way no other platform matches. But LinkedIn carries some of the highest ad prices of any major platform, and for winning architecture projects the reach and the economics almost always favor paid social on Meta, which is the engine we run for firms.

Keep LinkedIn for organic relationships and let the paid budget work where the cost per qualified inquiry is lower.

Company page or personal profile?

Personal, by a distance. Followers engage with a named human far more than with a logo, and a commercial client wants to see the person who would actually run their project. Keep the company page current so the firm looks alive, but put the real effort behind the principal's own profile.

I do residential. Is there any point at all?

Not for finding clients, no. For keeping the builders, designers, and realtors who refer you warm, and for hiring, yes, a little. Spend an hour a week there and put the rest of your attention where your buyers actually are.

How much time should this take?

A few posts a week from the principal, and no more. LinkedIn is a supporting layer, so it should never eat the time or the budget that belongs to the engine bringing you buyers on purpose. If it starts to, you have your priorities inverted.

Be in the right room

LinkedIn is a good tool held wrong by most of the firms using it. If you do commercial work, it is a real way to stay known to the people who make shortlists and send referrals. If you do residential, it is a staff room, not a shopfront.

Either way it is the layer on top, not the machine underneath. When you want the machine that actually brings buyers to your door, on a schedule you set instead of one you hope for, that is the part we build and run for architecture firms.
