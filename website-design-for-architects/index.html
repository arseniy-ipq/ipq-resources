Website design for architects

Most architecture websites are designed to impress other architects. Which would be a clever strategy if other architects ever hired you. They don't, so your most expensive marketing asset spends its life collecting compliments from competitors while the people with actual projects click around, find nothing built for them, and leave.

Arseniy VolosevichFounder, IPQmedia·Updated Jul 14, 2026·7 min read

Here is the twist: you already win the part everyone worries about. Researchers at Carleton University found visitors judge a site's visual appeal in about 50 milliseconds, and Stanford's web credibility work found 75 percent of people judge a company's credibility by its website design.

Beauty buys real trust, and architecture firms have the best-looking websites of any industry on the planet. You pass the 50-millisecond test with room to spare. It's the next ten minutes where the inquiry dies.

The short answer

An architecture website converts when it answers a buyer's real questions instead of impressing peers: one obvious next step on every page, an honest answer to the cost question, the process in plain steps, proof placed next to the ask, and pages that load fast on phones. Design earns trust in 50 milliseconds. The words are what turn a visitor into an inquiry, and the words are what most firms refuse to write.

The portfolio problem

A portfolio shows what you made. That is its whole job, and somewhere along the way it became your website's only job. A grid of projects named things like "Residence IV" (which tells a visitor nothing except there were at least three others), a Studio page with a moody black-and-white team photo, and a Contact link sitting at the end of the menu like an apology.

These conventions come from peer review. It is how firms signal taste to other firms, to juries, to the magazines. None of it is for the person who arrived with a project, a budget, and one question in their head: could this work for me, and what happens if I reach out?

That visitor is not a design juror. They do not know what "typology" means and they are not going to ask. When the site answers none of their questions, they do not conclude you are selective. They conclude you are expensive, or busy, or not for them, and they go inquire with a firm that explains itself.

A website is not a lead source

The most expensive mistake here is not bad design. It is the redesign as a growth plan. The pipeline gets thin, someone decides the website must be the problem, and months and a serious invoice later there is a newer, prettier site sitting in the same silence. The old site's problem was never how it looked. It had no visitors.

A website converts demand, it does not create it. Demand comes from the engine you put in front of it: paid ads putting your work in front of the right buyers before they search, and search catching the ones already looking.

The reverse mistake costs just as much and hides better. Run good ads, pay real money for a click from exactly the right buyer, then land them on a homepage that offers a photo grid and a hidden menu. The ad did its job. The site fumbled the handoff, and the report says "ads don't work" when the truth is the ads worked and the landing didn't. (The fumble is priciest on search clicks that run seven to twenty dollars each, numbers in Google Ads for architects.)

Engine and landing are one system, and the firms that grow fix both sides instead of blaming one. That system is the whole of lead generation for architects.

Two buyers, reading the same site for different things

If you do residential work, your visitor is usually a homeowner about to spend more than they have ever spent on anything, with no idea how the process works. They are scanning for reassurance. What happens when I reach out, what does an architect actually cost, will you laugh at my budget, what is it like to work with you for a year.

They read your photos differently than a jury does too: they are not admiring your restraint with materials, they are trying to picture their family in the kitchen. The site that answers the nervous questions out loud beats the prettier site that keeps its mystery. More on that buyer in marketing for residential architects.

If you do commercial work, your visitor is a developer or business owner building a shortlist, often comparing you against three or four firms in one sitting. They are scanning for capability and risk. Have you done this building type, at this scale, on budget, on schedule, and can you prove it. One case study with a real outcome (delivered on time, leased ahead of plan, built to the number) beats ten gorgeous photos with no story.

If they have to dig for your sector experience, you are off a list you never knew existed. That buyer gets the full playbook in marketing for commercial architects.

What a site that converts actually has

None of this requires touching your aesthetic. It is mostly words, placement, and answering questions you already answer on every first call.

  • One obvious next step on every page. Not a Contact link hiding in the menu, a visible invitation exactly where an interested reader runs out of page.
  • The cost question answered. How your fees work, what drives them, honest ranges if you can. Everyone arrives with this question and almost every firm refuses to touch it.
  • The process, explained. What happens after they inquire, in plain steps. The unknown is what keeps people from filling in the form, and it is free to remove.
  • Proof where the decision happens. A client's words or a result placed next to the ask, not exiled to a testimonials page nobody visits. The public version of the same proof is your review profile, covered in Google Business Profile for architects.
  • Case stories, not captions. The situation, what you did, how it turned out. Feeling-forward for residential, numbers-forward for commercial.
  • Speed, especially on phones. Google found 53 percent of mobile visits get abandoned when a page takes over three seconds to load. Your ad traffic is mostly on phones, so the nine-second autoplay drone film is mostly admired as a white rectangle.
  • A short form, answered fast. Name, contact, a little about the project. Then a reply that arrives while they still remember you, which is the first email in email marketing for architects.

The traps that look like good design

  • The mystery navigation. A hamburger menu on a desktop screen, because apparently telling people where things are is gauche. Elegant for a jury, a dead end for a buyer.
  • The awards strip as a substitute for outcomes. Buyers do not know what the ribbons mean. They know what "finished on budget" means.
  • The interrogation form. Eleven required fields, including "how did you hear about us," which is a bold thing to demand from someone you have not earned a hello from yet. Every field you add filters out real inquiries.
  • The silent homepage. Headlines like "Space. Light. Form." read as poetry to peers and as a riddle to a buyer who wants to know what you do and for whom.
  • The redesign every few years while the words never change. New grid, same missing answers. If the site did not convert before, a fresh coat of minimalism will not start it.

A few honest answers

Do I need a full redesign?

Usually no, and be suspicious of anyone whose diagnosis is always the thing they sell. Most firms get further by keeping the design and fixing three things: a clear next step on every page, a real answer to the cost question, and load speed on mobile. Days of work, not months.

Won't talking about fees scare clients away?

It scares away the people who were never going to hire an architect, which is a service to your calendar. The serious buyer already has the question, and silence does not make your number smaller in their imagination. It makes it bigger. The firm that gives honest guidance first usually gets the call first.

My site is beautiful. Isn't that enough?

Beauty gets you looked at, and the research above says it earns real credibility. But credibility without a next step just produces admiration, and admiration does not sign contracts. The visitor thinks "stunning work," closes the tab, and hires the firm that told them what to do next.

Fix the handoff, then feed it

Your website is half a machine. It turns attention into inquiries, and every fix above makes it waste less of the attention you pay for. The other half is getting qualified buyers onto it in the first place, on purpose, every week.
