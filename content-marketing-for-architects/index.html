Content marketing for architects

People ask Google what an architect does about 9,900 times a month. They ask what one costs a further 720 times. The average firm blog answers neither, because it is busy announcing an award to an audience of other architects.

Arseniy VolosevichFounder, IPQmedia·Updated Jul 14, 2026·7 min read

Content marketing arrives with baggage, because most firms have only met the fake version: a blog that died at post three, a newsletter last seen in 2023, and a page called Insights holding two staff promotions and some thoughts on materiality.

The real thing is simpler. Content marketing means answering the questions your future clients are already asking, in public, with the numbers you would give them across a table, so the trust normally built on a first call is built before anyone calls. In the system mapped in architecture marketing this is the trust layer, and the layer firms get most wrong. Which is good news, because the bar you need to clear is lying on the ground.

The short answer

Content marketing for architects means answering the questions your future clients already ask Google ("what does an architect do" runs about 9,900 US searches a month, "how much does an architect cost" another 720), in public, with the numbers you would give across a table. One honest page a month, written from real projects, beats twelve announcements. It earns trust before the first call and keeps earning search traffic for years after.

The questions your buyers are already typing

We pulled the real US search numbers rather than guessing. "What does an architect do" runs about 9,900 searches a month. "How much does an architect cost" gets about 720, with "how much does it cost to hire an architect" adding 140 more. "Questions to ask an architect" gets about 170. "How to choose an architect" is only 50, but advertisers pay around $19 for one click on it, which tells you what the person typing it is worth.

Some of the 9,900 are students picking a degree. Plenty are not. And almost none of these questions are answered by an actual architecture firm. They are answered by content mills, directories, and cost-calculator sites that have never designed anything. Your expertise is being explained to your future clients by people who do not have it. That should annoy you into action.

If you do residential work, the person typing those questions is a homeowner at the start of the biggest purchase of their life. They research for months before they contact anyone, quietly, at night, mildly terrified of getting it wrong. Answer honestly what it costs, how long it takes, and what can go wrong, and you become the firm that told them the truth while everyone else showed them photos.

If you do commercial work, your buyer asks different questions. "Cost to build commercial building" pulls about 320 searches a month, with quieter feasibility, timeline, and permitting questions stacked behind it. But your most important reader is not browsing at all. They are a developer or business owner vetting a shortlist with your name on it, and your content is what proves you have delivered their building type, at their scale, on budget.

Why the blog you already tried went nowhere

Here is the honest benchmark. Orbit Media surveys several hundred marketers about blogging every year, and in the 2025 edition only 21 percent said their content produces strong results. That is the hit rate for people who write for a living. Firm blogs run worse, and the reason is not effort or talent. It is direction.

The typical firm blog is a press office. Studio shortlisted for award. New project architect joins the team. Reflections on courtyard typologies. All of it written for peers, staff, and competitors checking whether you are busy. No buyer has ever typed "reflections on courtyard typologies" into Google. The blog answers questions nobody asked, gets read by nobody it was meant for, and the firm concludes content does not work.

What to publish instead

1. One page, one question, real numbers

Take a question a client asked you last month and answer it in public with the numbers you would give them in a meeting. What full service costs as a share of construction. What actually happens between signing and breaking ground. Where budgets usually blow up.

The specifics you consider obvious are what buyers cannot find anywhere else, and "obvious to you, unavailable elsewhere" is the whole definition of content worth publishing. It is also what earns quotes and links from real publications, the game covered in PR and press for architects.

2. Case studies that read like proof, not poetry

A portfolio shows the building. A case study shows the decisions, which is what buyers actually shop for. For a residential reader: what the family wanted, what the site made difficult, and the part where it nearly went sideways. For a commercial reader: the budget, the schedule, and what the building returns.

Numbers do the persuading. Adjectives are what firms reach for when they do not have numbers. Where those stories sit on your site, and what has to surround them before they convert, is website design for architects. The same case study shot as a short walkthrough is the most-watched version of it, which is video marketing for architects.

3. A schedule, not a mood

In Orbit's data, the marketers who publish more often are the most likely to report strong results, and about half of them manage two to four pieces a month. You need less. One genuinely useful page a month beats twelve announcements, and at the surveyed average of roughly three and a half hours per post, it costs one long afternoon. Firm blogs rarely fail from bad writing. They fail at post three, when inspiration was the plan.

4. Let AI draft, never author

The math is tempting: a model can generate fifty posts by Friday. Orbit's survey found only about one in ten marketers let AI write complete articles, and that group was the least likely of any to report strong results. Google's spam policies target scaled low-value content by name. Use AI to outline and tighten if it helps. The thing that makes a page unbeatable is the twenty years of site visits in your head, and no model has been to your site visits.

Where content stops working

Content compounds, and compounding is slow. A page published today starts earning search traffic in months, not weeks, and the honest ranking timeline lives in SEO for architects. Until then a page mostly works as sales collateral. More fundamentally, content reaches people already looking for answers. It cannot make eight qualified buyers appear just because March is slow. No amount of publishing gives you that dial.

A few honest answers

We post finished projects on Instagram. Is that content marketing?

It is a habit, and a decent one, but organic reach is mostly your followers, which for most firms means peers and past clients. The buyers who have never heard of you sit on the same platform and are reachable on purpose through the paid side, which is a different tool doing a different job. Keep posting for the record. Pay to reach strangers. (The professional-network version of the posting habit is LinkedIn for architects, genuinely useful for commercial firms, a staff room for everyone else.)

How long does content marketing take to work?

Months for search traffic, days for everything else. The week it goes up, a good case study can close the lead who was on the fence, feed an ad campaign as proof, and answer half the questions on your next first call. Judge content as a trust asset first and a traffic source second, and it starts paying from week one.

Who writes it, the principal or a hired writer?

The knowledge must come from the principal. The typing can come from anywhere. A writer who has never sat through a planning objection cannot fake your answers, and readers can smell it when they try. The worst option is outsourcing the thinking, which is how firms end up with a blog of generic design listicles that could belong to anyone, and therefore belongs to no one.

Publish what only you know

Your future clients are asking Google thousands of questions a month and getting their answers from websites that have never built anything. Every month that stays true is unclaimed ground. One honest page a month claims it. And when you want those pages wired into an engine that brings buyers on purpose instead of waiting for them to search, that is the work we take off your hands.
